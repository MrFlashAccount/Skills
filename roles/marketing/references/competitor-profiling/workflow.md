# Competitor Profiling Workflow

## 1. Start from the decision

Name why this profile work exists:
- choose positioning angle
- prepare comparison pages
- support launch or campaign framing
- arm sales against objections
- understand market gaps and threats

This keeps the profile from turning into trivia collection.

## 2. Collect evidence in layers

### Layer A: product surface
Capture what the competitor says about itself:
- homepage promise and audience clues
- navigation / solution taxonomy
- pricing and packaging
- demo, onboarding, or signup friction
- feature pages and integrations
- proof surfaces: logos, case studies, testimonials, benchmarks
- changelog / release notes / product direction signals

### Layer B: market response
Capture what others say:
- review themes
- common praise
- recurring complaints
- migration or switching stories
- community discussions and reputation signals

### Layer C: comparison relevance
Map what matters downstream:
- where they are strong
- where they are weak
- who they fit best
- who they likely do not fit well
- what objections they neutralize well
- what claims they make that require verification before reuse

## 3. Normalize every competitor

For each competitor keep the same fields:
- company / product name
- URL
- category framing
- target audience clues
- core promise
- pricing / packaging snapshot
- notable capabilities
- proof surfaces
- common objections / complaints
- differentiators they push
- likely switch triggers
- strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, threats
- evidence notes and open questions

## 4. Claim discipline

For each important statement, classify as:
- **Verified**: directly supported by current source material
- **Inferred**: likely true but synthesized from multiple signals
- **Unknown**: cannot be supported safely yet

If the work feeds public comparison pages, avoid repeating inferred items as facts.

## 5. Comparison readiness

A profile is ready for downstream use when it can answer:
- who this competitor is best for
- why someone buys them
- what pain they solve well
- where they disappoint or create friction
- how we differ credibly
- what proof we have or still need before claiming advantage

## 6. Output options

### Single competitor profile
Use for deep-dive research or one strategic rival.

### Cross-competitor matrix
Use for page planning, battlecards, or category positioning.

### Differentiation brief
Use when the main job is to sharpen market angle, not archive every detail.

## 7. Handoff contract

Every output should end with:
- safe comparison claims now
- claims needing more validation
- missing proof on our side
- recommended next owner: positioning, copy/comparison, launch, or sales enablement

# Pattern Families: Cards and Containers

Use this file when judging whether the surface should group content through cards, spacing, dividers, or more open composition.

## When cards are justified

Use cards when:
- elevation or containment communicates real hierarchy
- a block needs to feel like one actionable or conceptual unit
- the surface benefits from clear separation between modules with different jobs

Avoid cards when:
- spacing, headings, and dividers can create a cleaner rhythm
- every section becomes a box out of habit

Why it works:
- cards can make ownership and emphasis obvious
- they help isolate actions, status, or mixed-content modules when needed
- softer rounded or superellipse-like corners can make containment feel more authored and less commodity-default when that matches the product language

Failure mode:
- card spam that makes everything feel equally important and visually heavy
- square or under-rounded containers that make the surface feel harsher or more generic than the rest of the composition wants

## Divider-led grouping

Use when:
- the content is sequential or tabular
- the surface is dense and should stay operational
- hierarchy is better expressed by order and spacing than by enclosure

Why it works:
- reduces visual chrome
- keeps data surfaces lighter and faster to scan

Failure mode:
- under-grouping that leaves users unsure where one section ends and another begins

## Hard anti-pattern reminder

Equal generic three-column card rows are forbidden as a default pattern.

Reason:
- they flatten hierarchy
- they are one of the strongest generic-ai tells
- they are too often chosen from habit rather than because the content actually wants that shape

Exception threshold:
- only when the content is genuinely uniform, comparison across all three items is the point, and a different structure would reduce clarity
- even then, treat it as a justified exception, not the default move

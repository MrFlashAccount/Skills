# Pattern Families: Layouts

Use this file when the question is about macro-composition, information rhythm, or which overall page structure best fits the surface.

## Split hero / asymmetric hero

Use when:
- a marketing or launch surface needs a strong first-screen payoff
- one side carries narrative and the other carries proof, product, or imagery

Avoid when:
- the surface is documentation-heavy or tool-dense
- both sides fight equally for attention with no clear primary read

Why it works:
- creates immediate hierarchy
- breaks generic centered-hero sameness
- gives narrative and proof distinct jobs

Failure mode:
- both halves become noisy and neither wins

## Bento / asymmetric tile grouping

Use when:
- different content blocks have genuinely different weight or purpose
- the product benefits from curated scan order rather than uniform repetition

Avoid when:
- every tile says roughly the same thing
- the layout is being used only to look trendy

Why it works:
- lets hierarchy show through size and placement
- prevents repetitive card rows from flattening the surface

Failure mode:
- novelty grid with no reading logic

## Narrative section pacing

Use when:
- a marketing surface needs section-to-section progression
- the page should feel authored instead of template-cloned

Avoid when:
- the surface is primarily a dense utility workflow

Why it works:
- creates momentum and confidence
- reduces component repetition fatigue

Failure mode:
- every section uses the same structure with different copy

## Dense utility stack

Use when:
- the surface is a dashboard, admin panel, or tool screen
- scan speed and operational clarity matter more than visual theater

Avoid when:
- the product needs storytelling, persuasion, or slow exploration

Why it works:
- keeps important controls and signals near each other
- respects the working rhythm of repeat users

Failure mode:
- utility density turns into clutter because hierarchy is weak

# Cold Email

Use this skill for outbound emails to people who did not ask to hear from you.

The job is not just writing clean copy. The job is matching the right buyer, trigger, value bridge, proof, and ask tightly enough that the email feels relevant and easy to answer.

## Read order

1. Read `../../shared/go-to-market-context/README.md` when product, ICP, pains, proof, or message boundaries are not already explicit.
2. Read `../../roles/marketing/ROLE.md`.

## Routing boundary

Use this skill when the main artifact is outbound email copy or outbound-sequence strategy:
- first-touch cold emails
- founder-led outreach
- SDR / AE prospecting emails
- outbound follow-up sequences
- subject-line and CTA iteration
- account or segment-specific outbound angle testing
- teardown of underperforming cold emails

Do not use this skill when the main job is:
- lifecycle, nurture, onboarding, win-back, launch-follow-up, or product-triggered email flows -> `lifecycle-email`
- landing pages, campaign pages, or site copy -> hand off to the appropriate copy/docs owner
- broader sales collateral, objection docs, or deck messaging -> hand off to the sales owner after this skill produces the outreach brief

## Inputs to gather

Capture the smallest set of facts needed to write something real:
- target buyer, company shape, and why this account or segment matters now
- trigger or research signal
- painful before-state you are pointing at
- concrete value bridge from that pain to the offer
- proof, credibility, or believable constraint
- desired reply motion and any compliance or brand limits

If inputs are thin, do not block by default. Write the strongest version possible, then name the missing facts that would most improve relevance.

## Workflow

1. **Classify the ask**
   - single email
   - short follow-up sequence
   - teardown / rewrite
   - angle pack for a segment or account list
2. **Build the outbound brief**
   - who this is for
   - what trigger or observation earns the interruption
   - what problem hypothesis the email is leaning on
   - what proof can safely be used
   - what low-friction ask fits the audience
3. **Choose the shape**
   - observation -> problem -> proof -> ask
   - trigger -> implication -> proof -> ask
   - question -> value -> ask
   - freeform if the note reads more naturally that way
4. **Draft the asset**
   - 2-4 subject options when subject lines matter
   - the email body
   - follow-up angles if the ask includes a sequence
5. **Run the quality gate**
   - does it sound human, specific, and peer-level?
   - is the personalization actually connected to the problem?
   - is there exactly one ask?
   - did we avoid fake urgency, feature dumping, and generic praise?
6. **Package next-step notes**
   - testing ideas
   - missing proof or segmentation gaps
   - handoff notes for sales, docs, or DevRel if another surface must carry the same message

## Output contract

Return only the pieces that matter for the request. Typical outputs:
- outreach brief
- subject-line options
- first-touch email
- follow-up sequence with per-touch angle and delay
- rewrite notes explaining what changed and why
- list of missing proof / research that would materially improve performance

When useful, structure sequence output like this:

```md
Audience:
Trigger:
Primary ask:

Touch 1 - [angle] - [timing]
Subject:
Goal:
Copy:

Touch 2 - [angle] - [timing]
...
```

## Handoffs

- **Sales owner**: when the email exposes objection patterns, qualification gaps, or talk-track needs that belong in a broader outbound playbook.
- **Docs owner**: when the email promise depends on a setup guide, migration doc, comparison page, or proof asset that does not exist yet.
- **DevRel owner**: when the audience is developer-heavy and trust depends on repository, API, or technical-first framing beyond a short outbound email.

## Rules

- Keep the email short enough to justify the interruption.
- Lead with their world, not your company bio.
- Personalization is only valid if it changes the reason for reaching out.
- Use proof carefully; never invent customer names, numbers, or certainty.
- Prefer interest-based asks over premature meeting grabs.
- Each follow-up must add a new angle, not a bump.
- Preserve open questions instead of masking weak targeting with louder copy.
